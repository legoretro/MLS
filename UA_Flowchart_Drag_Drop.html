G
Section 1

Glomerular Disorders

Glomerular disease starts at the filtering membrane. When that barrier is injured, urine commonly shows blood, protein, and casts.

Start Glomerular membrane injury Immune damage, membrane thickening, deposits, or inherited barrier weakness.
Inflammatory pattern Think glomerulonephritis when blood and casts are prominent.
blood + protein
Post-strep GN RBCs, dysmorphic RBCs, WBCs, protein, RBC casts; serum ASO may be elevated.
RPGN, Goodpasture, vasculitis Proteinuria with RBCs and RBC casts; renal function can worsen quickly.
Membrane or deposit pattern The membrane becomes thickened, damaged, or loaded with immune deposits.
watch protein
Membranous or membranoproliferative GN Proteinuria and RBCs; membranoproliferative disease may include decreased complement.
IgA nephropathy or chronic GN Hematuria pattern; chronic disease can add protein, glucose, and reduced renal function.
Protein-loss pattern The main clue is heavy protein loss with edema or lipid findings.
fat clues
Nephrotic syndrome Marked protein, RBCs, fat droplets, oval fat bodies, RTE cells, fatty or waxy casts.
Minimal change, FSGS, Alport, diabetic nephropathy Protein is important; RBCs or inherited basement membrane changes may help separate causes.
Big UA clueBlood plus protein points back to the glomerular filter.
Microscope clueRBC casts and dysmorphic RBCs make the glomerulus feel likely.
Protein clueVery heavy protein with lipid findings fits a nephrotic pattern.
T
Section 2

Tubular Disorders

Tubular disease affects the cells that reabsorb, secrete, or respond to signals. Clues often involve RTE cells, epithelial casts, low specific gravity, or substances that should have been reabsorbed.

Start Tubular cell or reabsorption problem Tubules are injured or cannot handle filtrate normally.
Cell injury pattern Ischemia or toxins injure renal tubular epithelial cells.
RTE cells
Acute tubular necrosis Mild protein, RTE cells, epithelial casts; hyaline, granular, waxy, or broad casts may appear.
Proximal reabsorption failure Several filtered substances are lost because proximal transport fails.
many losses
Fanconi syndrome Glucose, amino acids, phosphorus, sodium, potassium, bicarbonate, and water can be affected.
Specific response or transporter problem One function is mostly affected instead of the whole proximal tubule.
specific clue
Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus Tubules do not respond to ADH; urine has low specific gravity with frequent urination.
Renal glycosuria Urine glucose is increased even though serum glucose is normal.
Big UA clueRTE cells and epithelial casts point toward tubular cell injury.
Reabsorption clueUnexpected urine glucose or multiple losses can mean transport failure.
Concentration clueLow specific gravity with frequent urination fits the ADH-response branch.
I
Section 3

Interstitial Disorders

Interstitial disease affects the space around tubules, so inflammation and infection clues matter. WBCs, bacteria, WBC casts, and eosinophils help separate the branches.

Start Interstitial inflammation or infection Inflammation around tubules often overlaps with tubular findings.
Lower urinary tract pattern Bladder infection without kidney cast clue.
WBC + bacteria
Cystitis Numerous WBCs and bacteria, mild protein, and RBCs.
Upper urinary tract pattern Kidney involvement adds cast clues.
WBC casts
Acute pyelonephritis Numerous WBCs and bacteria, mild protein, RBCs, plus WBC casts.
Chronic pyelonephritis WBC casts can appear with granular, waxy, or broad casts and long-term damage.
Drug or allergic inflammation pattern Inflammation appears without the bacteria pattern.
no bacteria
Acute interstitial nephritis RBCs, protein, many WBCs, WBC casts, eosinophils, and absence of bacteria.
Big UA clueWBCs and bacteria suggest infection somewhere in the urinary tract.
Upper tract clueWBC casts separate kidney involvement from simple lower UTI patterns.
Allergy clueEosinophils with no bacteria fits acute interstitial nephritis.
